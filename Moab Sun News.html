      Oil and gas companies could soon pay far less to lease public land, including around Moab

      Ryan Sutherland/Bureau of Land Management-Utah (public domain)
      Moab Sun NewsJuly 21, 20262 mins
      Listen to audio version of this article
      Sponsored by
      0:00 / 2:28
      Instaread

      The federal government is proposing to let oil and gas companies put down a much smaller deposit before leasing public land — down to a fraction of what's required today. The policy change would also allow, for the first time ever, charging a fee to formally challenge a lease sale. Here's what's changing, and how to weigh in before the August 24 deadline.

      The Bureau of Land Management (BLM) manages most of the public land around Moab, including land leased for oil and gas drilling. Before a company can lease that land, it has to put down a deposit — called a bond — that covers cleanup if the company walks away without doing it. This is a nationwide rule, not something aimed at Moab specifically — it applies to every BLM oil and gas lease in the country.

      If this policy change passes, the deposit for a single lease would drop from $150,000 to $10,000 — a 93% cut. The deposit covering all of a company's leases statewide would drop from $500,000 to $25,000 — a 95% cut.

      Advertisement

      Filing a protest against a lease sale is currently free — there's no fee at all. The new rule would charge $1 per page for any protest longer than 50 pages, the first fee of any kind BLM has ever charged to protest a lease sale.

      Why this isn't just paperwork for Moab

      The BLM has already sold land right here twice this year. On July 14, it signed off on 39 parcels through the Moab and Vernal field offices. Back in April, it sold 58 parcels across the Vernal, Moab and Monticello offices, including land in Grand County. A third sale is already scheduled for September — 35 more parcels, again through the Moab and Vernal offices. Whichever rules are in place by then would be the first to apply.

      The National Parks Conservation Association and other conservation groups argue the changes would weaken protections near Canyonlands National Park and other public land.

      The BLM says the 2024 amounts were too expensive for smaller companies to afford, and that it can still ask any one company for more money if that company has a bad track record. This follows the BLM's rollback of the Public Lands Rule back in May.

      How to weigh in

      You have until August 24 to submit a comment. Go to regulations.gov and search docket BLM-2025-0037. Comments filed after that date might not count.
