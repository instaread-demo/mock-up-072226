‘The most environmentally conscious building is one that already exists.’ – Councilmember Rick Cole

Cole makes the case for missing middle housing at City Talk forum.

by Suzanne York
April 15, 2026
4 mins read

0:00 / 6:22
Instaread

In a wide-ranging Earth Month conversation, Pasadena City Councilmember Rick Cole sat down with veteran planner Tony Perez of Opticos Design to examine an increasingly central question to Pasadena’s future: how the way we build our city affects the environment, and how it might evolve in the years ahead.

The discussion, part of Cole’s monthly “City Talk” series, moved from the scale of individual buildings to entire neighborhoods, ultimately focusing on what these ideas could mean for Pasadena at a moment of significant change.

From Building Codes to Climate Impact

Perez, who began his career in city planning in the 1980s, described a profession that has undergone a quiet but profound shift. While environmental regulations once felt procedural, he said, they now shape the core of building design.

“Over the last 15 to 20 years, it’s become integral,” Perez explained, pointing to California’s energy efficiency standards, commonly known as Title 24, as a major driver.

Cole illustrated that transformation with a personal example: a recently built accessory dwelling unit (ADU) on his property that relies entirely on electricity, uses solar power, and costs just $700 annually in total energy use. Features like high-performance insulation, double-paned windows, and heat pumps significantly reduce energy demand.

Both speakers emphasized that sustainability is not just about new construction.

The Case for Adaptive Reuse

One of the strongest themes of the discussion was the environmental and cultural value of adaptive reuse: repurposing existing buildings rather than demolishing them.

“The most environmentally conscious building is one that already exists,” Cole noted.

Perez agreed, adding that reuse preserves not only “embodied energy”, the energy already invested in materials and construction, but also a community’s sense of identity. Pasadena, with its historic architecture and preserved neighborhoods, offers numerous examples of buildings that have found new life as restaurants, housing, and civic spaces.

“People love that a building didn’t go away,” Perez said. “It still contributes to the neighborhood.”

This approach, Cole suggested, often resonates emotionally with residents even more than its environmental benefits.

Beyond Buildings: Designing Walkable Communities

While individual buildings matter, both speakers stressed that the larger environmental impact lies at the neighborhood level, particularly in transportation.Buildings account for roughly 40 percent of greenhouse gas emissions, Cole noted, but transportation, especially car dependency, remains a major contributor.Perez described a growing national shift toward “walkable places,” a concept that has gained traction over the past decade. These neighborhoods reduce reliance on cars by placing housing, jobs, and daily amenities within walking distance or near transit.

He highlighted a striking example: a car-free residential development in Tempe, Arizona. Built without parking spaces and designed around shaded courtyards and narrow pedestrian pathways. The project demonstrates how design can dramatically reduce both energy use and heat.Temperatures in the development’s courtyards can be 25 to 30 degrees cooler than surrounding streets, thanks to building placement and passive cooling strategies. Residents rely on walking and nearby transit rather than cars.

“It’s not just theoretical anymore,” Perez said. “It’s happening- and people love it.”

A Pasadena Opportunity: The 710 Corridor

The conversation turned to Pasadena’s own once-in-a-generation opportunity: the former 710 freeway corridor. After decades of conflict over a proposed freeway extension, the land, long a scar through established neighborhoods, has been returned to the city. Plans now envision transforming it into a mixed-use, environmentally sustainable district. Cole described the site as a chance to create Pasadena’s first “carbon-neutral neighborhood,” integrating housing, green space, and walkability while addressing historic injustices associated with the freeway’s original route. Perez supported the vision, noting that the scale, potentially 1,800 housing units across roughly 50 acres, allows for a mix of building types and densities.

“This is where Pasadena can lead,” he said, suggesting that a blend of smaller, neighborhood-scale buildings and larger structures could create a more cohesive and human-scaled environment.

Growth Pressures and Community Concerns

Yet the path forward is not without tension. California’s housing laws now require cities to accommodate higher-density development, often resulting in 5- to 7-story buildings with podium parking. In Pasadena, these projects have sparked concern among residents who feel they clash with the city’s character.

Cole acknowledged the challenge: balancing the need for more housing, particularly near transit, while maintaining the qualities that make Pasadena unique. Perez noted that this tension is playing out across California. State density bonus laws, designed to encourage affordable housing, can override local planning efforts, sometimes leading to projects that exceed community expectations. Still, he praised Pasadena’s newly adopted objective design standards, which aim to shape development through clear, visual rules rather than abstract metrics like density or floor-area ratios.

“These are about making buildings look and feel like buildings people want to live near,” Perez said, pointing to standards that emphasize features like windows, entrances, and street-level engagement.

Rediscovering the “Missing Middle”

Another key solution discussed was “missing middle housing”, a range of housing types that fall between single-family homes and large apartment blocks. These include duplexes, fourplexes, bungalow courts, and small apartment buildings – many of which already exist in Pasadena’s historic neighborhoods but were effectively banned by zoning rules for decades. Cole described these housing types as essential for offering more choices while maintaining neighborhood character.

“Americans expect options,” he said. “And this is about expanding those options.”

Integrating such housing into existing neighborhoods remains sensitive, particularly in historic areas. But Perez suggested that careful design and alignment with existing lot sizes can allow these forms to blend in seamlessly.

Moving Forward

As the discussion concluded, both speakers struck a cautiously optimistic tone. Despite regulatory challenges and community concerns, Perez sees a broader national trend toward more sustainable, walkable, and diverse communities. Cole framed the moment as an opportunity for Pasadena to lead – not by resisting change, but by shaping it.

“We’re trying to find a Pasadena way,” he said, “to accommodate growth while enhancing quality of life.”

The stakes, both agreed, extend beyond housing or design. They touch on climate, equity, and the fundamental question of how cities can evolve without losing what makes them livable. As Pasadena continues to grapple with these issues, from the 710 corridor to neighborhood infill, the ideas explored in this City Talk forum suggest that the future of the city may depend not just on how much it builds, but on how thoughtfully it does so.
