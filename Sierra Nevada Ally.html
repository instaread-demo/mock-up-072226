Beyond the Strip: How Las Vegas bands are finding their stage at local businesses

Las Vegas is known for its glamorous array of entertainment, extravagant venues and big-name performers. However, when it comes to the music scene, there is much more to the city than trendy concert halls and household names.

By Liliana Padilla
●
Community
●
July 21, 2026

The smell of paint and clay gives way to guitar amps and drum kits at the back of Clay Paper Ink. The small paint-your-own pottery studio, located seven miles south of the Las Vegas Strip, is lined with shelves of delicate ceramics, art supplies and works in progress. Hidden beyond them is an unexpected stage for local bands and artists.

Since October 2025, Clay Paper Ink has hosted performers about twice a month. The venue welcomes all kinds of music, but has become a hot spot for metal and alternative rock.

One of those bands is All Day Affair, a Las Vegas-based post-hardcore band. The group has been together for about a year, making changes and fine-tuning its sound. Its multitalented members take turns singing and playing different instruments.

“My favorite venue so far would have to be Clay Paper Ink. It felt pretty cool performing in an art shop,” said Edwin of All Day Affair.

The studio pushes aside their painting tables to make way for moshing and dancing. Some of the big venues on the strip don’t allow mosh pits. Smaller venues allowing fans to mosh really makes the experience complete. It’s like getting to feel the music and properly enjoy it.

The underground music scene is vast and Las Vegas hides many small-scale venues in surprising locations throughout the city.

On legendary Fremont Street, punk’s not dead–it’s just inside of Evel Pie, an old-school pizza shop in old downtown.

“We’re very edgy, punk rocky, kind of in your face, ‘Here’s some pizza, now sit down and eat it,'” Evel Pie manager Miller said.

Evel Pie is an homage to ’70s Americana, and as the name implies, also a tribute to Evel Knievel and the spectacle of his attempted jump over the Caesars Palace fountain. From the entrance to the backyard, the walls are covered with murals depicting Knievel’s death-defying jump.

In the backyard, decked out with string lights and scattered amplifiers, local bands perform for crowds of loyal fans. Visitors get to leave their mark on the venue, participating in the scene by attending shows, supporting local artists and adding to the ever-growing collage of stickers that covers the walls.

“The bands usually bring in a lot of people, so before they even start, we get a nice rush from it, and then it definitely helps out business throughout the night,” said Miller.

It’s more than just good business. These venues offer more than just a fun way to spend a weekend. They give emerging bands a chance to build an audience. Inertia is one local band that has taken to the backyard stage. Formed in 2024 by four friends, Inertia has become part of the city’s growing local music scene.

These spaces, albeit unconventional, give artists like Inertia a chance to do that. And at times, it goes beyond music.

Evel Pie and other venues will sometimes provide space for Punk Rock Saves Lives (PRSL), an organization that offers on-site bone marrow registry drives, mental health resources and other community services at concerts and events. Local chapters attend punk shows, connecting with concertgoers through music while helping people access resources and register to potentially save lives.

“A lot of people probably are familiar with seeing them at punk rock bowling warp tour and other festivals like that, so that’s the national crew that you’ll see for the bigger events, and you’ll see us at all the little ones,” Liz of the PRSL Las Vegas chapter said. “We’ve had small local shows where we were more successful in getting people to join the bone marrow registry than we were at shows with maybe ten times the attendance, just because the community is so supportive of one another.”

These uncommon venues seem to be win-win-win. It’s good for bands, local businesses, and the community. Yet, how do bands find these hole-in-the-wall businesses to partner with?

Many artists connect through local booking organizations, including Vegas Underground. That organization prioritizes emerging bands trying to find their footing in the local music scene and make it easier for smaller acts to get on stage. Performers can reach out through the organization’s website or social media, and Vegas Underground handles the rest.

Vegas underground also promotes the shows for the bands online and on social media. This is how a majority of fans find out about performances. If you’re trying to find shows in small venues around Las Vegas, they’re easy to find when you search on social media. 

“The booking process as a band is pretty simple because there are a bunch of bookers and promoters already so all we have to do as a new band was just hit up these people and we got a show,” said Gallardo. “A lot of these promoters mainly look for new local bands, giving everyone a shot to perform.”

Small venues create community, small artists are driven by a love of music and a connection to their fans. Being in one of these spaces you can feel a bond through the music, and the adoration in the air.

“I’m really passionate about music. I believe it’s something we need,” said Inertia lead singer Lance. “Without music, where would we be?”

So, the next time you’re looking for something to do on a Friday night, avoid the usual spots and skip the Strip. You might just find your next favorite band in the back of a pottery shop or behind a neighborhood pizza parlor.
